IT Technologies

  1. Cloud, Services and Servers

    2. Cloud computing in simple terms is a network of computers and servers that are connected like an array and behave as one computing system.
    There are 3 different categories of services:

    • IaaS(Infrastructure-as-a-Service) - it provides users with computing capacity via a server or many servers with storage. The customers are usually companies with IT expertise or recently, researchers. They need large amounts of computing power without the high cost, set-up, and maintenance of on-site servers.
    • PaaS(Platform-as-a-Service) - it supports writing software for computer systems, allowing businesses to write software for existing applications or develop customer application.
    • SAAS(Software-as-a-Service) - it provides software to businesses, this part of the Cloud is the largest and most developed. It is either one or a cluster of programs that are available within the Cloud and not a user’s hard drive.

    Cloud computing was first introduced in 1963 when DARPA requested MIT to create a model which allows a computer to be used by two or more people simultaneously. At the time, this was when computers were using magnetic tapes as memory. Then in 1969, a man named Joseph Carl Robnett Licklider helped develop a ‘primitive’ version of the internet and promoted the idea of connecting people from computer to computer which allows access to information from anywhere. Virtualization was introduced in the 1970s, it is a virtual machine which acts like a real computer with an operational OS. which lead to the development of the modern cloud computing infrastructure.

    Cloud computing in the early 2000s began to get more complex as more businesses such as Amazon were starting to gain
    a better understanding of its capabilities. As they began to use the infrastructure model, later on, other organizations followed on.

    Amazon then launched “Amazon web services which offer online services to other websites and users, which includes cloud-based storage, computation, and AI. They also allowed users to rent virtual computers and allow them to use their own programs.

    Today cloud computing is diverse, convenient, flexible and is everywhere. It changes everything about how we approach different needs, actions and processes that were once done via analog or from our own premises.

    Examples of cloud computing today include:

    • Cloud storage services (Dropbox, GoogleDrive) - offer varying storage spaces and prices which offer data redundancy and security.
    • Cloud gaming services (Nvidia RTX servers, Vortex) - allow users to play any game they offer on any device with little to low latency.
    • Audio/Video streaming services(Spotify, Netflix) - contains vast archives of music, movies, and shows which allow people to view or listen to them, on-the-go and on any device that supports it.
    • Render node services(RebusFarm) - with servers that contain hundreds or thousands of CPUs and GPUs, it allows users who are in the film and photography industry to utilize power in order to render their projects. Especially for VFX and 3D modeling.
    • AI/Machine Learning(Google Cloud Machine Learning Engine) - it lets developers and data scientists build and run advanced machine learning models and AI. Its applications are versatile, to identify clouds in satellite imaging, food safety, and prediction-based tasks.
    • Large-scale science experiments/research(Hadron Collider) - allows scientists and researchers to store large amounts of data and use computer processing to analyze petabytes of data. It’s also used as render nodes to 3D model and analyse particle collisions and particle energy analysis.

    Again, these are just some of the uses of cloud computing today that many businesses, researchers and computer/software engineers that break down and innovate ways of using this platform.

    --> What is the likely impact?

    The biggest impact that cloud computing in the industry is data storage and cybersecurity, as more industries are leaning towards the cloud as their main source of storage, it emphasizes the need for high-level security to protect against theft.
    There are also some benefits of storing their data on cloud-based computing:

    • Cost - there is no longer a requirement for businesses to build servers on their premises, building, updating and maintaining servers costs are very high and most companies don’t want to invest in that amount unless it’s needed.
    • Scalability - for small businesses, they may not have the real estate or money to get a server or they may feel that investing one that may overscale their needs. With cloud-based storage, they only need to pay for what they need and are able to scale up or down over time.

    There are 5 levels for cars that contain a type of self-driving feature described by researchers of the field:

    • Level 0 - All major systems of the vehicles are controlled by a human
    • Level 1 - Under certain conditions, the controls either the steering or the speed of the vehicle This includes cruise control or automatic braking. The driver performs all of the other critical functions and are responsible to monitor the road and take over if the system were to fail.
    • Level 2 - The cars offer at least two or more simultaneous automatic functions, like acceleration, steering and braking under certain conditions. But it requires a driver in order for it to be legally on the road and the vehicle can be safely operated. Tactical driving, processes and scanning for hazards are all responsibilities of the driver.
      • Example: Tesla Autopilot
    • Level 3 - The cars can manage all of the safety and critical functions under certain conditions. However, they require a driver in order for it to be legally on the road when the autonomous vehicle can’t navigate certain scenarios.
      • Example: Audi Traffic Jam Pilot
    • Level 4 - The car is fully-autonomous in some driving scenarios defined by factors such as road type or geographic area. For a privately-owned car, the driver may need to manage the driving factors on the urban streets but can be a passenger when it enters a highway.
    • Level 5 - A legal, autonomous vehicle that can operate on any road and condition a human driver can navigate. The driver or passenger only needs to put the directions of their destination.

Retina:
In simpler terms, the part of our eye that translates an image into electrical signals is the retina. Retina however can be divided into two main photoreceptors namely Rods and Cones. These are both cell types which are both light sensitive and are used for two varied purposes, rods are for monochrome vision in the dark, and cones for detection of high details in presence of light. When light strikes either type of cells, it is converted into electrical signals.

Now comes the question of details. Human eyes have approximately six million cones. These cones are of three types: Blue, Green and Red. Colours that we see today from black to white and everything in between are caused as a result of these three types of receptors. For example, the colour yellow results from green and red cones being stimulated while the blue cones receive no stimulation. The brain interprets these signals into the colours that we see.

In essence, what we can gather from this is that the image that has been captured has to be converted into these three main distinct signals so that our brain may convert the signals into our desired images. Whilst there must also be a way to control the brightness much like our eyes that is done by adjusting the amount of light entering into the external input of our visual aid capturing the pictures.

Eye contraption, i.e. Camera:

Cornea:
The first stage that enables our eyes to receive light is the cornea. It is the transparent structure found at the very front of our eyes that helps focus most of the light much like the lens of a camera. Behind the cornea is an iris, a coloured ring-shaped membrane with an adjustable circular aperture which expands and contracts regulating the amount of light entering the eye.

While much of this is more or less like a camera, the challenge itself is the sending and receiving of signals from the brain in order to control the iris like structure within the contraption to give a more or less seamless feeling for our users so that no extra stress falls upon them while using our product. Action Potential (Spike):
The action potential also known as spike is a brief electrical signal that occurs in neurons, muscle, endocrine and plant cells. This is often referred to as the neuron "firing". Embedded within the membrane of a neuron, there is a series of protein channels that allow small charged protein and potassium ions to travel in and out in an orchestrated pattern down the length of the entire axon. This occurs by the protein channel slightly changing in shape when a certain threshold is reached, which is usually around 15 millivolts above the 70 millivolt resting state. This triggers an all-or-nothing action potential which then travels the length of the axon.

All-Or-Nothing i.e. Binary:
All stands for 1 and Nothing stands for 0. Since 70 millivolts is the resting state, it is safe to assume that the value would be our binary equivalent of 0 and 15 milli volts higher than that would be our 1. Interestingly, parents, siblings and children of people with schizophrenia show some of the same abnormalities to a lesser degree. David Schretlen, Ph.D., a professor of psychiatry and behavioral sciences at the Johns Hopkins University School of Medicine, reasoned that this type of brain stimulation might ease some of the cognitive difficulties that afflict people with schizophrenia giving them a shot at a normal life.

In a research done with 11 participants, five of them were adults with confirmed schizophrenia and the other six were their close relatives. Each participant received two 30-minute treatments one using a negative electrical charge, which the researchers thought might prove beneficial while and the other using a positive charge as a control. During and after each treatment, participants completed a battery of cognitive tests. On tests of verbal and visual working memory, participants performed significantly better after receiving a negative charge, and the effects were surprisingly strong.

In another test, participants were given a minute to list things they could buy in a supermarket. Most people taking the test rattle off items in categories, naming fruits, vegetables, dairy products, for example, it was found that while participants did not rattle off more words, they did better at the challenging task of switching between categories after a negatively charged treatment.
